Andrej Karpathy
AI researcher · former Tesla, OpenAI

LLMs become your personal wiki compiler, maintainer, and research agent.

01
Key signals
  • ◆ Ephemeral agent teams spawned per query — each LLM call can orchestrate multiple agents to construct a wiki, lint it, iterate, output reports. Far beyond simple text completion.
  • ◆ Knowledge bases at ~100 articles + 400K words scale don't need fancy RAG yet — frontier LLMs auto-maintain indexes and summaries well enough to synthesize across documents.

What this means for you

This is your template for building knowledge-driven AI products. The pattern: ingest → compile (LLM writes structured markdown) → query (agent-style reasoning over the KB) → output (rendered, visual, re-filed). Watch how Karpathy ditches RAG complexity at small scale — that's a business insight. Room for a product here means room for you to build the UX layer on top of this workflow. Start with one domain (your sales funnel, competitor intel, domain research) and watch how fast it compounds.

Aaron Levie
CEO · Box

AI agent architecture is a treadmill: build scaffolding, models improve, tear it down, repeat.

02
Key signals
  • ◆ Ruthlessly unsentimental refactoring is the core loop. As models improve, the mitigations you built (chunking, data retrieval hacks, constraint systems) become performance ceilings, not floors.
  • ◆ Frontier capability leaps make yesterday's guardrails into overfitting. Box Agent had to evolve multiple components between design and release because model improvements rendered prior scaffolding harmful.
  • ◆ The trap: getting nostalgic about tech you've built. The win: always redesign for today's frontier capabilities, not yesterday's model limitations.
What this means for you

As you build AI products, expect this cycle — your v1 scaffolding will look primitive in 6 months. Don't fall in love with your architecture. Instead, build review rituals: every quarter, ask 'which of my safety rails, data processing, or constraint systems are now performance drags?' The winners will be the ones shipping fast, measuring against frontier models, then stripping away complexity. This also means your product roadmap should assume models improve faster than your custom code.

Ryo Lu
Design Lead · Cursor

Glass over black box — agents visible, diffs editable, you stay in control as AI gets stronger.

03
Key signals
  • ◆ Terminal was black box (one line in, few out). AI kept the black box but made it addictive (wish → pull lever → accept/reject whole). Glass breaks the box — every agent move, diff, and plan is visible and editable.
  • ◆ Same tool, infinite depth, your surfaces. PM sees the plan materialize. Designer iterates live in code. Engineer reads diffs, learns, builds intuition. The tool adapts to how you think, not vice versa.
  • ◆ Control compounds quality. Best work happens when you can intervene, catch what the model missed, add your taste. The agent gets better because you're thinking alongside it, not because it's thinking for you.
What this means for you

Cursor 3 just shipped this philosophy and it's a signal for where your products should go. If you're building anything agent-adjacent, the premium move is transparency + control, not 'make it fully autonomous.' Users want to see the reasoning, tweak the plan mid-execution, and own the outcome. That's also your defensibility — products that hide the work get commoditized. Products that let power users see and shape the work become indispensable.

Peter Yang
Product · Roblox, AI educator

Cursor 3's new simple-first interface beats the old button-heavy default — prioritize talk-to-agent over configuration.

04
Key signals
  • ◆ Old Cursor interface had too many buttons and toggles; cmd+shift+p unlocked the real UX. The new version makes simple-first the default view.
  • ◆ Implication: Users prefer conversational, agent-forward interaction over knobs and settings. Configuration should hide until needed, not compete for attention.
  • ◆ Design lesson: The best feature is often the one that's hardest to find because it shouldn't be visible until the user needs it.
What this means for you

Apply this to your AI products immediately. Your default surface should be the conversational agent, not a settings panel. Bury advanced options behind a clear gesture (cmd+shift+p, a drawer, a toggle). Test: if your users aren't finding your power features because they're too prominent, you've overexposed configuration. Cursor's move validates that simplicity wins, especially as agents get smarter.

Kernel security reports jumped from 2–3/week to 5–10/day since Jan '26 — mostly AI-generated but correct, overloading maintainers.

05
Key signals
  • ◆ AI slop in security reports is now at industrial scale — ~100x increase in volume. The shift: reports are actually correct, so they can't be auto-dismissed.
  • ◆ Maintainer burden exploding. Open-source projects now need dedicated triage resources to process AI-generated but legitimate vulnerability reports.
  • ◆ This will kill some OSS projects that lack the bandwidth to handle 50+ real vulnerability reports per week.
What this means for you

Signal for two things: (1) If you're building on OSS foundations, expect the maintainer ecosystem to slow down — review cycles will lengthen as they drown in AI-generated reports. (2) If you're selling to enterprises, this is a sales opening — enterprises will pay for triage and verification automation over raw human review. Consider building a security report prioritization product.

Autonomous labs will disrupt biotech for the first time — AI makes biology programmable at scale.

06
Key signals
  • ◆ 30 years of tech (Internet, social, back-office IT) didn't touch biotech. This is different. Autonomous labs fundamentally change how science and biopharma operate.

What this means for you

This isn't directly your space, but watch it as a category signal. Autonomous labs = AI orchestrating physical processes at scale. The product pattern mirrors your knowledge-base work: design (prompt/DNA code) → execute (automated foundry) → test (cell growth, measurement) → iterate. If you're thinking about AI products that move atoms (manufacturing, biotech, materials), Ginkgo's 2008–2024 journey shows the capital, timeline, and patience required. But also: the market is real and the disruption is coming. Consider whether your AI skills apply to physical-world automation.
